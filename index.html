With Campuses Open, College Towns Hope For a Boom. Can Students Afford the Skyrocketing Prices?

By James O'Donnell

September 28, 2021

Supply chain disruptions mean a rise in prices for both students and parents .

Photo by Fabian Blank on Unsplash

NEW YORK - Millions of college students are returning to in-person learning for the first time in over a year, and they'll face some of the highest prices the economy has seen in 13 years.

College towns and cities, heavily reliant on the traffic of students for their restaurants, bars and shops, are expecting an economic boost. But it’s not clear if students will be able to keep up their spending habits of the past.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics reports economy-wide inflation of 5.1% since last August. Supply chain issues like labor shortages and transportation delays have driven up prices in specific sectors. While the price hikes of used cars (up 31.9%) and gas (up 42.7%) have grabbed headlines, everyday items for students are also on the rise.

From a beer at the bar to an airline ticket home, here’s what price changes students are facing this fall.

While Federal Reserve policies in the pandemic did lead to an increase in the money supply, experts say that the price hikes are mostly caused by supply chain disruptions.

Those supply chain issues are sweeping. A shortage of truckers has raised transportation costs and causes delays for manufacturers, and a delay on microchips has sunk production worldwide for most auto makers, with Toyota announcing earlier this month a 40% cut to its global production. The list of shortages runs long: diapers, lumber, cardboard. Most experts expect the delays to continue at least through the end of the year.

Some retailers are passing the costs of those supply chain issues onto customers.

The Wall Street Journal reported that Dollar Tree will be selling more items above $1 in response to rising costs, and Kroger indicated on an earning calls in early September that the company will be passing rising costs along to consumers "where it makes sense to do so.

Federal Reserve Chairman Jerome Powell emphasized to lawmakers on Sept. 30 that he is confident that inflation will taper off. Their response to the pandemic is evolving: Chairman Powell signalled that stimulus programs may wrap up this fall, and inflation rates could begin to increase. A rise of interest rates would be a sign to investors and businesses that the end of the pandemic is near.
